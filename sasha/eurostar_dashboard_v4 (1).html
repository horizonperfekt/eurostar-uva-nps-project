Two critical delay thresholds
Threshold 1 · 19.5 min: detractor territory begins
Past 19.5 minutes the typical passenger tips into detractor territory. Predicted NPS drops from 46.3 to 33.2, a 13.2-point loss (-28%). Promoter share falls around 7 percentage points.
Threshold 2 · 46.5 min: NPS near zero
Past 46.5 minutes predicted NPS falls to 0.3 (-99% from on-time). The 26.5-46.5 min window is where recovery pays most: roughly 1 NPS point per minute pulled back.
Controllable failures cost the most
Rolling Stock: worst penalty at every delay level
Above 26.5 min, Rolling Stock failures predict NPS of 11.0, which is 10.6 pts below the delayed-service average of 21.7. This group covers M1 Reliability (rolling stock breakdowns), M2 Depot Release (late departure from depot), and M3 Trainset Shortage (fleet unavailability or substitution). All three are within Eurostar's control.
Weather & Traffic: low penalty
External causes predict NPS ~2 pts below average at >26.5 min (-9%). Passengers accept these delays differently. Actionability is limited.
Fleet & maintenance are actionable
Equipment change · -9.73 NPS
An unplanned equipment change knocks 9.73 points off predicted NPS per affected service (95% CI: -11.5 to -8.0). Better turnaround planning and rolling stock reliability can prevent most of these.
RUB fleet (newer Continental) · +11.73 NPS
RUB trains (newer Continental fleet, N=6,214) predict +11.73 NPS vs TGH (Continental reference) (95% CI: +8.9 to +14.5), a +25% lift. This is a fleet-age effect: RUB is newer than TGH. Effect is uniform across delay levels, not interaction-dependent.

Early journey delay: separate compounding effect
Detected via SHAP interaction analysis and decision tree thresholds · ordered logit confirmation pending
Onset: 13.5 min
Early journey delay starts costing NPS from 13.5 minutes. Decision tree and SHAP both pick up the same threshold. It adds to arrival delay rather than replacing it.
Plateaus around 50 min, unlike arrival delay
Early journey delay damage levels off around 50 minutes. Arrival delay keeps falling all the way to 300+ min. Recovery effort past 50 min of early delay returns very little. The money is in the first half hour.
Both thresholds at once: worse than adding them up
When both delays cross their thresholds together, the combined NPS hit runs ~10-15% above what you'd predict from adding them separately. SHAP interaction rank 2 of all operational pairs (mean abs = 0.032). Ordered logit Models F/G still to run.

Headline finding
Rolling Stock failures cost the most at every delay level, and they're the group Eurostar has the most control over. Rolling Stock = M1 Reliability M2 Depot Release M3 Trainset Shortage. The two cheapest groups (Weather, Traffic Regulation) are externally driven. Passengers don't hold those against the operator in the same way.

Trainset shortages hurt NPS before any delay is recorded. Passengers on services running with substitute or shortage-affected stock score lower even on punctual journeys. The deficit appears to reflect the experience quality of substitute stock as much as any disruption signal. Compensation: non-separable from delay effect
H2 test · delay >60 min compensation collinearity finding
Can't separate from delay_gt60
In this dataset, every journey delayed more than 60 min received compensation; none below that threshold did. That makes delay_gt60 and compensation receipt the same signal. There's no way to isolate the compensation effect independently with this data.
What to do with this
There are 331 zero-compensation journeys in the >60 min subset, too few to get a clean separation (0.19% of that band). H2 should be closed as "non-separable from delay_gt60 in current data." Isolating the compensation effect properly would need a future dataset with partial coverage across the threshold.

What this section covers
Current Maintenance (CM: open faults), Preventive Maintenance (PM: days since last service), and cleaning variables. All ranked by SHAP from the XGBoost operational_only model. No ordered logit CIs for these individually. Treat SHAP rank as relative importance. The individual effects are modest; they compound across the journey.
Preventive maintenance (PM): SHAP importance by class
Days since last PM inspection · operational_only XGBoost · ranked by Detractor SHAP
pm_days_since_catering is the fourth-ranked operational Detractor driver, behind arrival delay, early journey delay, and disruption. Catering PM recency carries a stronger SHAP signal than most current-fault variables. The physical condition of catering systems appears directly visible to passengers. Comms and toilet PM follow.
Current maintenance (CM) open faults: SHAP importance
Open CM defects by system · price_and_staff XGBoost · ranked by Detractor SHAP
WiFi faults top the CM ranking for both Detractor and Promoter classes. It's the most visible fault type. Passengers notice immediately, and the effect runs in both directions. Interior, catering, and climate faults follow. Total open faults captures overall system condition rather than any specific failure.

SHAP interactions involving CM/PM variables
operational_only XGBoost · mean absolute SHAP interaction value · top three pairs involving CM/PM variables
Arrival delay × toilet PM · 0.0043
Poor toilet maintenance amplifies the NPS hit from delay. A disrupted journey on a train with overdue toilet maintenance is measurably worse than either factor alone.
PM catering × PM interior · 0.0038
Overdue catering and interior maintenance compound each other. Keeping one system current while neglecting the other doesn't capture the full gain. The scheduling needs to be consistent across domains.
Arrival delay × comms PM · 0.0037
Delayed journeys on trains with overdue comms/entertainment maintenance score worse than the arithmetic would suggest. Whether that's reduced distraction, failed communication during the delay, or both is hard to separate from this data.
Connecting passengers: priority protection segment
SHAP interaction analysis · ordered logit interaction model pending
Baseline disadvantage
Passengers with onward connections score 2.19 points lower at baseline (CI: -3.3 to -1.0). That gap grows past 25 minutes once connection risk becomes real. SHAP shows stronger negative values for this group in the 25-50 min delay range.
Recommended intervention
Real-time delay alerts, automatic rebooking triggers, and proactive compensation once arrival delay exceeds 25 min. Connecting passengers are the segment where service recovery effort returns the most NPS per passenger reached.

Passenger motivation: delay tolerance differs
SHAP dependence plots · XGBoost · replicated across feature sets
Brand-loyal passengers (prefer train)
Brand loyalty partially cushions moderate delay penalties. NPS effect is additive with reason_quickest: combined loyalty = 8.67 vs single = 8.05-8.24 vs neither = 7.49. The two variables work independently. There's no interaction between them (corr = -0.184).
Captive passengers (no viable alternative)
Score lower at baseline and show a floor effect. Extra delay minutes don't compound as strongly once these passengers are already dissatisfied. The dissatisfaction looks structural (no viable alternative, price perception) rather than operationally driven. Mechanically distinct from brand-loyalty buffering.
Staff perception: moderator of operational effects
SHAP analysis · XGBoost price_and_staff · staff_composite = 5-point composite of onboard staff ratings
Strongest monotonic relationship
Staff composite has the cleanest monotonic relationship in the dataset: SHAP values run from -3 to +1 across scores 1-5 with no threshold kinks. In the price_and_staff model, staff_composite is the top driver for both Detractor (SHAP 0.537) and Promoter (0.375) classes, well above any operational variable.
Delay amplifier: asymmetric effect
Low staff scores make delay more costly. Staff × arrival_delay is SHAP interaction rank 3 (0.024 mean abs) in the price_and_staff model. The floor (avoiding Detractors) is driven by operations. The ceiling (reaching Promoters) needs good staff scores to open up. The two mechanisms are distinct.
Partial mediator: total vs direct effects
Adding staff_composite to the ordered logit (result_36) shrinks several operational coefficients, which is what you'd expect if staff perception partially carries the delay effect (through communication and service quality under disruption). The model without staff gives total operational effects; with staff, it gives the direct effects net of that channel.

Key data quality notes
Confirmed structural artifacts from preprocessing
clean_score_routine: Continental imputation
62,119 rows (35% of N) are exactly 0, all Continental. These aren't real zero scores. It's an imputation artifact. Only Channel-only analysis is valid. The same 62K rows are also missing pm_days_since_catering.
total_pax: same Continental missingness
62,119 Continental rows lack total_pax. The apparent low-occupancy inverted-U was a Continental missingness artifact and has been retracted. The high-occupancy effect (>400 pax, Channel-only) was recoded as a binary breach dummy and has been removed pending further research.
disrup_type_encoded: Qualtrics artifact
Survey-routing artifact, same problem as metadata_disrup. gbm_final.pkl, which includes it, is superseded. gbm_clean.pkl (R²=0.2104, 80 features) is the canonical model.
delay_is_internal / external: three-state variable
These are not complements: corr = -0.378, and 39% of rows are neither internal nor external. Kept for descriptive framing only, not as interaction terms. The delay_is_internal × arrival_delay interaction does not replicate in XGBoost and has been removed.
Model performance summary
Seed sensitivity across 10 seeds (101-110) · holdout validation
price_and_staff R² = 0.272 ± 0.009 across 10 seeds (range: 0.256-0.281). operational_only R² = 0.120 ± 0.011 (range: 0.105-0.134). The holdout seed-42 result (0.257) was a low draw; the headline figure is 0.272. The 0.152-point gap between feature sets reflects the combined contribution of price perception and staff quality, consistent across all 10 seeds.
