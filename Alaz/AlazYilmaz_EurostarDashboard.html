Staff Service is the only variable that strongly buffers delay — excellent staff performance is the primary lever for NPS recovery during disruption.
SHAP Global Feature Importance — Mean |SHAP| per Feature
SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) computed via TreeExplainer on XGBoost model (n=2,000 test-set passengers). Mean |SHAP| = average absolute SHAP value across all passengers = average magnitude of each feature's contribution to individual NPS predictions. Unlike tree-based feature importance (which counts splits), SHAP captures the direction and magnitude of impact per passenger.
#FeatureMean |SHAP|% of TotalType
Staff Service dominates with Mean |SHAP| = 0.505 — more than 4× the next feature (Train Cleanliness at 0.338). Arrival Delay ranks 4th (0.170) — below three survey variables, confirming that perception matters more than operational delay. The interaction terms (Delay × Service Quality) together account for ~15% of total SHAP importance, evidence of the moderation effect documented in the thesis.
SHAP Moderation Analysis — Does Service Buffer or Compound Delay?
For each service quality variable, we compare the SHAP gap (high service score vs low service score) at zero delay vs high delay (>5 min). Buffering = gap widens under delay → service matters MORE when delayed → protects NPS. Compounding = gap narrows under delay → service matters LESS when delayed → cannot rescue NPS.
Staff Service has the strongest buffering effect (+0.244): at high delay, the gap between high and low staff scores is 24.4% of an NPS point larger than at zero delay. Catering shows the strongest compounding effect (−0.240): under delay, the impact of catering quality collapses — passengers disengage from food service when frustrated by delays. Implication: Prioritise staff training and responsiveness as the primary delay mitigation tool. Do not over-invest in catering upgrades as a delay recovery strategy.
SHAP vs XGBoost Feature Importance — Comparison
XGBoost feature importance counts tree splits (frequency of use). SHAP calculates the actual per-observation contribution. These can differ significantly — a variable used in many splits may have small average impact, while a rarely-split variable may have large per-passenger impact.
Arrival Delay moves from rank 3 in XGBoost FI (13%) to rank 4 in SHAP (0.170 mean |SHAP|), overtaken by WiFi. Catering drops in SHAP relative to FI — its tree splits are frequent but its per-passenger impact is moderate. SHAP provides a more honest picture of what actually drives individual NPS scores.

Robustness Check — Objective-Only Models
Models using only operational/objective features (no survey perception variables). Demonstrates the indispensability of perceived service quality in explaining NPS variance.
Removing all survey variables from XGBoost drops R² from 0.371 → 0.111 (−70%). Operational metrics alone explain only 11% of NPS variance. This is the empirical proof that service perception, not operational metrics, drives NPS.
