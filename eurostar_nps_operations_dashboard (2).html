The delay budget

delay → NPS · Methodology 1

Every minute of average delay costs 0.30 NPS points. The line crosses the target at 16 minutes — that is the budget each service has before it pulls the score down.

NPS falls 0.30 points per minute of delay

📌Finding: a punctual service scores ~42.8 and crosses the 38 target at 16 minutes.
✅Action: treat 16 minutes as the recovery trigger — once a live service passes it, prioritise it for platform, crew and pathway decisions.

Delay risk zones

severity bands

NPS does not fall in a straight line — it collapses between 30 and 60 minutes, then partially recovers past 60 once compensation reaches passengers.

The 30–60 minute red zone

Mean NPS of services by delay band · 95% confidence intervals · empirical
📌Finding: NPS bottoms at 13 in the 30–60 min band, then recovers to 26 beyond 60 min as compensation lands. The damage is worst before compensation currently triggers.
✅Action: bring compensation & passenger care forward to the 30-minute mark instead of waiting for 60. The proven recovery at 60+ shows it works — applying it earlier lifts the worst band.

Causes & blame

same delay, different judgement · Methodology 2

Comparing services with the same delay length, the cause still shifts NPS. All recorded causes shown, net of delay duration. Passengers forgive ambient traffic — they punish fleet failures and strikes.

Internal — within Eurostar's controlExternal
📌Finding: net of delay length, vandalism (−50), strikes (−35), trainset shortage M3 (−32), depot late release M2 (−22) are punished hardest; traffic regulation (+13) and knock-on pathway loss (+24) are forgiven.
✅Action: the three worst controllable causes are all fleet failures (M1/M2/M3). Prioritise them in maintenance and depot scheduling — see the next section.

The fleet failure modes

linking both methodologies

The single most important link in this project: the three operational levers from Methodology 1 each have a failure mode in the M-codes of Methodology 2 — and all three are heavily punished by passengers.

Reliability (R) → fails as M1 Fleet malfunction
1,661 services · 87 min avg delay · −16 NPS net of delay

Turnaround (T) → fails as M2 Late depot release
295 services · 80 min avg delay · −22 NPS net of delay

Availability (F) → fails as M3 Trainset shortage
361 services · 56 min avg delay · −32 NPS net of delay

📌Finding: in Methodology 1 fleet availability looked like a weak lever at today's reliability — but Methodology 2 shows that when availability fails (M3), it is the most heavily punished controllable cause in the network. Capacity is insurance, not a daily dial.
✅Action: protect against the failure modes, not the averages. Hold standby trainsets to prevent M3, tighten depot release discipline to prevent M2, and target preventive maintenance at the units driving M1.

How disruption spreads

propagation & the standby decision

A delay is contagious — a late-arriving train pushes out the departures behind it. This shapes one timetabling choice: run a spare train as a service, or hold it as a chain-breaker.

One disruption delays the next ~8 departures

Excess departure delay of following trains vs a punctual run · network propagation
📌Finding: the train right behind a disruption departs ~4 min later than it otherwise would, and the knock-on stays material for ~8 services — a footprint of ~28 delay-minutes spread down the line.
✅Action: recover the first disrupted train fast — stopping it early saves the whole tail of services behind it, not just the one.

Two networks, two problems

Channel vs Continental

Channel runs four times the delay but its passengers rate more generously; Continental runs cleaner but its passengers drop hard when disrupted — and only judge the cause on Channel.

Live disruption decision playbook

trigger → action → outcome

When a disruption hits, the decision depends on the predicted delay and the cause type. Each rule below fires at a measurable threshold.

Predicted delay
Under 16 min
Hold & run. Service is within budget — no special action; standard recovery.
Outcome: NPS stays ≥ 38. No compensation cost.
NPS = 42.8 − 0.30 × delay.
Predicted delay 16–30 min · any cause
Prioritise recovery
Give the service priority on pathway and crew. Begin proactive passenger information now.
Outcome: keeps NPS in the 30s; avoids slide into the red zone.
Each minute saved here = +0.30 NPS pts.
Predicted delay 20+ min · INTERNAL cause (M1/M2/M3)
Recover hard + own it
Throw recovery resource at it and communicate honestly. Deploy standby for M3; on Channel, state the cause clearly. Internal causes are the most punished.
Outcome: avoids the −22 to −32 attribution penalty stacking on top of the delay.
M3 −32, M2 −22, M1 −16 net of delay. Blame effect significant on Channel.
Predicted delay 30–60 min · any cause
RED ZONE — trigger care
Trigger compensation and passenger care immediately. Do not wait for 60. Activate hotel/taxi/food protocols early.
Outcome: lifts the worst band (NPS 13) toward the recovered 60+ level (NPS 26).
Band NPS: 24 → 13 (30–60) → 26 (60+, compensated).
Long delay · EXTERNAL cause · Channel
Communicate the cause
State the external cause clearly and early (weather, infrastructure, traffic). On Channel these are forgiven when correctly attributed; never let them read as a Eurostar failure.
Outcome: external causes recover toward neutral — traffic regulation scores +13 net of delay.
Attribution effect confirmed on Channel (p<0.001), absent on Continental.
Very long delay · cascading network risk
Cancel decisively
If a service will run extremely late and threatens downstream rotations, cancel cleanly and rebook rather than dragging a severely delayed service through the network.
Outcome: protects following services from knock-on delay; contains total network NPS loss. Pair with proactive rebooking.
Turnaround buffer limits propagation; a single very late service cascades into others.
First train in a disruption · dense day
Break the chain early
Prioritise recovering the first disrupted train, and on dense or high-risk days hold a trainset on standby to substitute in and stop the knock-on before it spreads.
Outcome: protects the ~8 following departures; saves the ~28-minute propagation footprint a single disruption creates.
A late arrival adds ~4 min to the next departure; the chain stays material for ~8 services.

Qualitative & quantitative benefits

Quantitative: a higher NPS lifts recommendation and repeat travel, the commercial driver behind the score; compensation spend is targeted to the 30–60 min band where it recovers the most NPS rather than applied late; standby-fleet investment is justified as insurance against the single most-punished failure mode.

Qualitative: disruption teams get a clear, shared rule for what to do and when; communication becomes network-specific and honest, protecting brand trust; maintenance and depot priorities are tied directly to passenger sentiment rather than set in isolation.

Model basis. Chained regressions on 27,800 linked services: operations → delay (CV R² 0.40) and delay → NPS (CV R² 0.10; cause model CV R² 0.19 → 0.27 with route). Estimates are conditional averages, not per-train forecasts. Attribution effects bracket between cause-only and route-controlled specifications.
